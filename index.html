Urban heat in Arnhem
About urban heat and its risks

Global warming has been studied extensively over the past decades and it is evident that it has been and will keep leading to an increasing number of heat waves, that are also progressing in duration and intensity (Katavoutas & Founda, 2019; Perkins-Kirkpatrick & Lewis, 2020; Zhang et al., 2020). Health-risk caused by heat is called heat stress and can lead to excess mortality, as was exemplified during the heatwaves in Europe of August 2003 (Br√ºcker, 2005). The elderly and other vulnerable groups in the population are especially at risk to experience heat stress (Baccini et al., 2008). Urban populations have a higher chance to experience problems caused by heat stress than rural populations because urban climate characteristics and urban heat islands (UHIs) intensify and lengthen the effects of heat waves (Katavoutas & Founda, 2019; Lemonsu et al., 2015; Venter et al., 2020). As urban morphological characteristics and material properties strongly influence the local urban climate and walkability of the urban pedestrian networks, these are worth looking into when one wants to mitigate heat stress (Lemonsu et al., 2015).
